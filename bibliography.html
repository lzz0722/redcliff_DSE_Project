Editorial Notes
Decisions, conventions, and scholarly justifications
Note on the Wen Zhengming supplement

The first 36 characters of the autograph handscroll (National Palace Museum, acc. no. 故畫000882) are not in Su Shi's hand. The original opening of the scroll was damaged at an unknown date after Su Shi's death. The missing text was supplied by the Ming-dynasty calligrapher Wen Zhengming (文徵明, 1470–1559), one of the most celebrated calligraphers of his era. Wen's hand is visibly different from Su Shi's — more delicate and regular, in contrast to Su Shi's bold, idiosyncratic brushwork. This supplement is flagged throughout the edition: on the Manuscript page, the first tile carries a warning badge, and the sidebar explains the circumstances of the supplement.

Note on the translation of 賦 as "Rhapsody"

The genre 賦 (fù) has been translated variously as "ode," "prose-poem," "rhapsody," and "poetic exposition." This edition uses "Rhapsody", following the convention established by David R. Knechtges in his translations of Han-dynasty fu. The term captures the genre's characteristic features: extended length, a mixture of rhythmic prose and lyrical verse, elaborate imagery, and philosophical argument. "Ode" (as in the Pindaric or Horatian tradition) is avoided because it implies a shorter, more unified lyric form with celebratory or devotional connotations that do not apply to the fu.

Two features of the fu are essential for reading Su Shi's texts. First, the genre has a long tradition of host–guest dialogue (主客問答, zhǔ-kè wèndá): a "host" and a "guest" exchange arguments, with the guest raising a philosophical challenge that the host must answer. This explains the structural role of "the Guest" (客) in both Rhapsodies — he is not an incidental character but a generic requirement (see the separate note on the Guest below). Second, the fu is a performative genre, originally composed for declamation at court. Its language is deliberately heightened: elaborate metaphor, sensory accumulation, and rhetorical amplification are conventions of the form, not signs of excess. The vivid imagery of moon, water, and wind in these texts operates within a tradition where such language is the primary vehicle of philosophical argument.

Note on the scholarly debate surrounding the Guest (客)

The figure of "the Guest" (客) who appears in both Rhapsodies has been the subject of extensive scholarly debate. Three principal interpretations exist: (1) the Guest is a real companion — a friend who accompanied Su Shi on the boat trip; (2) the Guest is a literary device, a projection of Su Shi's own existential doubt, following the ancient fu convention of host–guest dialogue (主客問答); (3) the Guest is deliberately ambiguous — both real and symbolic, a figure whose ontological status is part of the text's philosophical inquiry.

This edition does not resolve the question. Instead, the annotation system encodes multiple interpretive layers: each occurrence of 客 is tagged, and the editorial notes present all three readings without privileging one over another. The Guest tab on the Five Images page discusses this in full.

Note on the two English translations

This edition includes two English translations: Burton Watson (1965) and Stephen Owen (1996). No additional translations are included. Full translations are not reproduced due to copyright; only passage-level excerpts appear in the annotation layer.

The two translators represent fundamentally different approaches to classical Chinese. Watson, working in the mid-century Columbia tradition of Arthur Waley and Donald Keene, prioritises fluency and literary impact in English. His sentences are rhythmically polished, his vocabulary vivid, and his phrasing designed to read as natural English prose. The cost of this fluency is that Watson sometimes resolves ambiguities that the original leaves open: where the Chinese is compressed or syntactically strange, Watson tends to expand and clarify. His 山高月小 becomes "the mountains were tall and the moon tiny" — an intensification that makes the emotional register immediately legible to an English reader, but one that goes beyond what the Chinese literally says.

Owen, writing three decades later as a scholar of Chinese poetics at Harvard, prioritises structural fidelity and interpretive restraint. His translations preserve the parallelism, compression, and syntactic ambiguity of classical Chinese, even at the expense of English fluency. The same four characters become "the mountains were high and the moon small" — a rendering that maintains the balanced two-character-per-clause parallelism (山高 / 月小) and lets the reader feel the understatement rather than having the emotion amplified. Where Watson interprets, Owen often holds back — reproducing the original's openness rather than resolving it.

The decision to include both is an editorial argument, not a courtesy. By placing Watson and Owen side by side beneath the same Chinese passage, this edition makes visible the interpretive gap that every act of translation must cross. The reader sees, concretely, that the "same" text produces different emotional effects depending on the translator's philosophy — and begins to understand that translation is itself a form of scholarly interpretation. The Five Images page includes specific translation notes wherever Watson and Owen diverge in ways that affect the emotional register of the five core images.

Note on manuscript coverage

The Manuscript page displays only the First Red Cliff Rhapsody. No autograph manuscript of the Second Red Cliff Rhapsody is known to survive. The Second Rhapsody exists only in later transcriptions and printed editions.
